This ticket should be directed to someone from Condor for assistance.  We have been in touch with the MIT_CMS site admins outside of GOC and they have said they cannot identify the cause of the problem and would like external assistance to sort it out.  This ticket summarizes several weeks of correspondence between us (SBGrid) and them (MIT - Max and Wei).

MIT is running Condor.  They have a situation where they evict a lot of jobs.  They agree this probably shouldn't be happening -- either the jobs should be left to run, or the jobs that get evicted should never have been started.  They cannot tell what part of their Condor policy is leading to this situation (our best guess is that it comes from some combination of START and RANK policies in condor_config).  The observed behavior follows this pattern:

t0 submit to MIT
t0+10 execute
t0+12 evict
t0+13 execute
t0+14 evict
t0+16 execute
t0+18 evict

We have submitted lots of jobs to MIT.  At times they run correctly to completion, but at other times most jobs go through this execute/evict loop until they get de-queued from MIT and re-queued elsewhere.  MIT is not changing their configuration, nor are we, hence the belief it comes from some mis-configuration of their condor_config policy.  Below is an excerpt from a single representative job log.

Here are some details from a recent email to MIT:

What we don't understand is how the system works that our jobs are started then immediately evicted then started again, then immediately evicted again.  To me it would seem like you have a policy that says:

START = True

which means all queued jobs are started with equal priority

EVICT = ( JOB.VO != uscms )

which means as soon as our jobs are started, they are evicted by the other queued CMS jobs that did not get prioritized by the "unbiased" "START" statement.

If that is the case, perhaps you could adjust your START policy to favor queued CMS jobs.  There are a few other scenarios where the observed behavior could legitimately occur.  If my suggestion is what happens, then there are two concerns:

1. (from your point of view) starting and evicting jobs takes time on the worker node and occupies the head node while jobs are staged in and out.  If a job isn't going to get a reasonable chance to run to completion, it probably shouldn't be started.

2. (from our point of view) jobs are stuck at sites which keep looking like they will run them, but then repeatedly evict them.  We have to wait for these jobs to eventually run or get moved to another site, and this pushes out our "TTC" (time to completion) for a full job set.  In addition, it results in the site getting ranked lower (our scheduling policy penalizes sites that take a job but then fail to run it), and therefore getting fewer jobs in an "unfair" way.
