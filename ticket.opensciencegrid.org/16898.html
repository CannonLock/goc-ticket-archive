    Hi Kevin,

To my knowledge, the directory /tmp/NNN.vmpsched is not created by us (the GLOW VO).  It appears to be an initial working directory created for the job by the batch system at Vanderbilt.  Our end-user jobs do not leave anything behind in their working directory, because that is automatically cleaned up for us by the glidein pilot, assuming the pilot is not abruptly killed.  If the pilot is abruptly killed, we rely on the Vanderbilt batch system to clean up after us.  Does it?  In addition to that worry, the pilot itself may leave some of its own working files behind, even when it exits normally.  If the batch system cannot clean these up, then please let us know and we will ask the glideinWMS team if there is anything they can do to make a best-effort to leave nothing behind.  It is my belief that all batch systems in use on OSG provide some sort of per-job scratch area that is automatically cleaned up, but perhaps I am wrong.

Our users generally do not make use of $OSG_APP.  I believe this is a common pattern for opportunistic OSG VOs who have small frequently modified applications that fit in the scratch space provided on worker nodes.  We do try to make appropriate use of caching to avoid excessive use of the site WAN.  If you believe this pattern of usage is creating problems, we should have a discussion about it, perhaps in a broader forum that includes other VOs who do things similarly.

    Sep 3, 2013 03:32 PM UTC by Kyle Gross 
    Hi GOC staff,

We have put a temporary hold on the ability of the nanohub and glow VO's to run jobs at Vanderbilt.  Over the weekend we noticed that both of them were creating directories in /tmp, populating them with copies of the software they needed for their jobs, and then not deleting the directories when the job completed.  I have included sample output from one node showing the problem below.

There are several issues here, I think:

1) why are they even putting software in /tmp in the first place?  I thought $OSG_APP was where they were supposed to do that.

2) why aren't they checking to see if the software they need is already installed?  If they installed in $OSG_APP (which is GPFS mounted across our cluster) and then checked for the software installation there then this whole issue could have been avoided.  Instead, we saw several dozen versions of the same exact software installed in /tmp on a single node by different jobs landing on that same node.  It was so bad that when I went to clean up their mess that the rm took over half an hour!

3) of course, why in the world are they not cleaning up /tmp when their jobs are done?

4) they ultimately filled the root disk on several worker nodes here at Vanderbilt, which adversely impacted other users.  They are on hold - and will stay on hold - until we have confirmation that the above behaviors are corrected.

Thanks…
