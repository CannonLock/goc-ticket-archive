    May 29, 2012 09:48 PM UTC 
    The usage from the previous day, or even several days back, can be updated if the probe is slow to send data or if the database is behind of it's built-in record buffer.  The completion time of the job can be different than when the data reaches the central database at FNAL.  Sometimes by quite a bit.

The timezone for everything is GMT.

I believe there is a bug in graphtool.  We are investigating now.

-- by Derek Weitzel
    May 29, 2012 08:41 PM UTC by avery@.... 
    Hi Derek,

You say that as each job completes its CPU or wall time is assigned to
00:00 of the day it completed. That implies that when I look at the
2-week accounting in the late evening (after jobs start to appear in the
next day bin) and again in the morning, that only the hours in the
current day bin should have increased overnight. However, I notice
frequently that the hours for the previous day have also increased in
the morning. How is it possible that the previous day bin continues to
fill after the new day bin has started filling if completion time is the
only criteria? Is it because the data in the database is not updated at
the same time? BTW, what clock or timezone is being used to assign the
day bin of the completion? Is it GMT?

I am not exactly sure what you mean by "graphing program" because there
are two graphs that are displaying inconsistent views of the wall-hour
data, at least inconsistent to an observer who might expect the same
information to be plotted. The first one is the 15-day histogram, which
fills until the end of the current day, and then another day starts. (I
assume from your email that this graph starts at 00:00 of each day.)
There is a reported "mean hours" underneath, but I am not sure if that
includes the full 15 days. The second graph is the pie chart, which,
based on what you said, seems to plot 14 days because it is measured in
seconds from the current time and thus misses the data on the first day
that was entered as 00:00.

I strongly believe that the graphs should refer to the same data. For
me, that implies truncating the previous chart by 1 bin but still report
the integer number of days in the graph ("14 days" = 14 bins). If the
argument in the previous paragraph is correct, the total number of hours
in each graph would then be identical. The ordering of VOs or sites
under each graph would then be identical, which they occasionally are
not, leading to more confusion.

Thanks,

    Paul

-- by Derek Weitzel
    May 29, 2012 04:48 PM UTC 
    14 days disguised at 15 is due to a couple of reasons.  First, all summary data is mapped to the day it completed, but at 0:00:00.  So if we say we want from now back 14 days, that includes today, and 15 days ago since we are capturing a 'slice' of that day.

Second, the graphing program is smarter than the database.  It sees that there is 14 days, because there's 14 days (in seconds) of data given to it.  So it says what it thinks.

So, possible solution:
- We could modify the graphing to say how many buckets there are, rather than how much time it thinks is displayed.

Ashu - Let me know if anything I said is wrong, but this is how I understand it.

-Derek
