    Jun 24, 2015 08:46 PM UTC by Thomas Lee 
    I don't know how the service works or how you plan to use it, so I can't speak to whether the SPF record is necessary, but you requested it in this ticket: http://ticket.grid.iu.edu/25437

When email comes from an address at support.opensciencegrid.org, but has been relayed via email.freshdesk.com, spam filters will check the SPF record to make sure email.freshdesk.org is authorized to relay email for support.opensciencegrid.org.  There are lots of mail servers and clients that don't check, but unless there's an SPF record, those that do will probably flag the email as possible spam.

Apparently you have a choice:

1. Have an SPF record and an A record, and have things break periodically, but email will never be marked as spam
2. Have a CNAME without an SPF record, and changing IPs will never break the service, but email may be marked as spam
3. Just use opensciencegrid.freshdesk.com without aliasing, so changing IPs won't break anything, and email will never be marked as spam
4. Find another service that doesn't change its IPs without warning

    Jun 24, 2015 08:03 PM UTC by Thomas Lee 
    I'm not a DNS expert either, but one thing I do know is that you can use a CNAME to create a symbolic equivalency, and that's exactly what we had at first; support.opensciencegrid.org was translated into opensciencegrid.freshdesk.com, and everything was good.  But at some point, that CNAME record was deleted and a new A record was created that mapped support.opensciencegrid.org to a static IP address.  I don't know how it happened, though there is the possibility that there was some technical trouble in the IU DNS admins' system.  I'm not sure, though.

Anyway, "dynamic DNS records" exist, though there's no such thing as a special kind of DNS record called a dynamic record.  All that's happening is that when opensciencegrid.freshdesk.com's IP address changes (via DHCP, etc.), Freshdesk's DNS servers send out that change to the rest of the world.  Most likely the host regularly contacts their DNS server, sending it its current IP.  But that's all internal to Freshdesk and not the responsibility of anyone outside their company.  It was the change of the CNAME record to an A record that set things up to be broken, although nobody noticed yet because the IP hadn't changed.  Then, when Freshdesk's server changed its IP, things broke for real.

Once the A record is changed back to the CNAME record it's supposed to be, the problem will be solved.
