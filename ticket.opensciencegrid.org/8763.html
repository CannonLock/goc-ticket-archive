    Jun 25, 2010 10:29 PM UTC 
    Hi Mike,

Up to now it seems like you've been aiming at the accelerator simulation (which is what LHC@HOME is), which has never been the biggest need for computing that the LHC has (hence the regular "no new work" situation), just the easiest to adapt to BOINC.

The experiments are where the computing power is needed, but that tends to be much more IO-heavy. There's nothing to stop you putting Linux on these systems, downloading and maintaining an Open ScienceGrid software stack and making yourself available as a resource serving Virtual Organizations such as CMS or ATLAS (or CDF, or D0 which are both still searching actively for the Higgs). However, if you're running off a standard home-grade network connection I would expect you to blow your monthly quota fairly quickly.

It's an unfortunate fact that with High Energy Physics experiments, most of our analysis tasks, even the ones that do require lots of CPU, are also very demanding on I/O. The one exception is detector simulation which requires a substantially higher CPU - I/O ratio.

As an example, reconstructing a single t-tbar event in the CMS detector takes about 3.5s on an E5430 (uses one core). A batch of 300 events will produce about 200 MiB of data that have to be retrieved (taking under 20 minutes to produce) and operates on about the same amount of input data. The analysis program consumes about 1.5GiB of memory. One could run multiple jobs in parallel if you have the memory, but then you're multiplying the data transfer requirements by that factor.

We appreciate that you are interested in furthering the science cause in general and that of the LHC in particular. In the particular case of HEP though, raw CPU power is not sufficient to carry the day.

I see a couple of alternatives for you, depending on your interests:

1. Sell your systems.
2. Expand your horizons and your generous philanthropy (I am *not* being facetious, here) to include other sciences. BOINC projects are the no-brainer since they involve very little work for you. A portfolio including things like Einstein@Home, cosmology@Home, the World Community Grid, climateprediction.net and Rosetta@Home would go a long way towards helping those causes. The other solution is to become a member of the Open Science Grid and support as many VOs (Virtual Organisations) as you feel able. This is a significant amount of effort on your part however, and may be incompatible with your ISP's bandwidth provision.

I hope you find a way to channel your enthusiasm for science research and leverage your not-unimpressive resources to provide good science output, wherever that ends up being. I'm sorry that HEP in general and LHC in particular is not set up to take best advantage of your generosity: an enthusiasm for basic research and the universe's fundamental questions is rare enough these days.

If you do decide to go the OSG route, we would be happy to help you set up a small OSG site, just let us know.

Thanks again for your interest.

Best,
Chris.

    Jun 25, 2010 05:56 PM UTC by rynge@.... 
    Mike,

The step up from running BOINC is to set up an OSG Compute Element
(CE), and probably a Storage Element (SE). This will allow the LHC
projects to send compute jobs your way. However, we usually do not
recommend this for smaller resources. The reason is that there is some
people effort involved to setup and maintain this system (a good
estimate is 0.25 FTE, at least initially).

Another thing to keep in mind is that even if you have set up a
perfect CE and SE, the project might choose to not send any work. This
usually happens with the bigger projects as they have more complex
software stacks and requirements. So at the end of the day, you might
have either an empty cluster or one servicing mostly non-LHC jobs.

I'm not trying to discourage you, but I want to set expectations to
where you will not be disappointed after spending a considerable amount
of your time.

If you want to do more reading about setting up a cluster of your size,
see the Tier3 documentation:
https://twiki.grid.iu.edu/bin/view/Tier3/WebHome
And if you are interested in discussion this over the phone, I would be
available to do that as well.
