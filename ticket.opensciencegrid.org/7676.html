    Oct 29, 2009 08:38 PM UTC by Arvind Gopu 
    Joe,

I understand your pain! Indeed there are acronyms everywhere.  We were
guilty of coming up with a couple of them but most of them just are
inherited from the OSG and other grid computing projects :)

The WLCG flags are related to OSG being part of the Worldwide LHC
consortium, and that the two VOs (ATLAS and CMS) wanted to do different
things with respect to their participation in the WLCG.

There is an underlying expectation that owning VO (ATLAS in your case)
will keep their site-admins informed on the choices they have made but
it does not always end up being the case.

I will try to explain this stuff as best as I can. I tried calling you
at the xxx-2598 number (stored on your OIM profile) in a hope I can
explain all this stuff but could not catch you.

Also note that we have help text on the OIM help menu -- for example:

https://twiki.grid.iu.edu/twiki/bin/view/Operations/OIMRegistrationInstructions#Resource_or_Service_Registration

(incidentally, I notice this needs to be updated - I will pass that to
my team)

> Should this resource be part of WLCG Interop BDII?

Do you want your CE to be exposed to WLCG researchers via the separate
WLCG BDII? If this does not make sense then you should set it to false.
(I am pretty most ATLAS owned sites have this set to false)

> Should this resource be part of WLCG Interop Monitoring?

Do you want your RSV results to show up on WLCG monitoring systems like
SAM/GridView? And therefore show up on the RSV vs.WLCG report emails?

    (You asked about this)

> Should this resource be part of WLCG Interop Accounting?

Should Gratia accounting information be forwarded to WLCG accounting
systems?

> KSI2K Minimum
> KSI2K Maximum
> HEPSPEC Value
> Storage Capacity Minimum (in TeraBytes)
> Storage Capacity Maximum (in TeraBytes)

These are related to computing and storage resources promised by VOs to
the WLCG - please contact your VO manager to learn more.

https://twiki.grid.iu.edu/pub/Operations/RSVPeriodicReporting/Gridview_Service_Availability_Computation-1.pdf

The algorithm states that availability is the percent of time the
resource was in the "UP" state.  "UP" is defined to be the percent
of time when the last-run of all the critical test returned "OK".

The other states a resource may be in are "UNKNOWN" and "DOWN".
"UNKNOWN" is the percent of time when the last run of at least one
critical test returns "UNKNOWN".  "DOWN" is the percent of time when
the last run of at least one critical is a failure.

The resource status is computed continuously; if test X has a
failure at 12:05, and has a success at 12:08, then the resource's
maximum availability for the time interval 12:00 to 1:00 is
(60-3)/60=95%.  There is no "minimum granularity".

A resource's availability is determined by the status of all the
registered services.  If they are all UP, the resource is UP.  If
just one service is UNKNOWN, the resource is UNKNOWN.  If just one
service is DOWN, the resource is DOWN.

A resource's reliability is:

(Availability) / (Scheduled Availability)

where the Scheduled Availability is

100% - Scheduled Downtime - Percent of time in state UNKNOWN

The following CE tests are marked as critical:
* cacert
* ce-perm
* osg-version
* ping
* gram

The following SRMv2 tests are marked as critical:
* srmcp
* srmping

All values in the below table are percentages; if no tests were run,
it is
marked with NT.
