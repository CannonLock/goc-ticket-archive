    Jul 9, 2014 02:54 PM UTC by Scott Teige 
    I spoke with Keith Lehigh, local security contact, about this yesterday. He will whitelist 129.79.53.179 on our end. He assured me that because of this our security people will not be contacting your security people in the future. Basically, if he does not know about it, he will take no action.

For completeness and internal reference, the current whitelisting mechanism is all or nothing. There are multiple automated detection mechanisms that can trigger an alert for security. The behavior observed here, while normal operation for this service, was diagnosed as a portmapping attempt. Keith will implement finer granularity in his whitelisting mechanisms, a machine can be listed to allow, for example, portmapping but still trigger an alert via another detected attack. If the need arises or is anticipated other machines can easily be added to the whitelist.
    Jun 30, 2014 04:07 PM UTC 
    Thanks, Vince. I haven't gotten a response from knock@.... yet to my email on Thursday, so I'll just follow up here — I'm glad the blocks were short duration; that explains why we weren't able to figure out what was blocked. The email from IU's abuse contact to uchicago's abuse contact didn't mention a limited duration, and there was no followup email indicating that it was lifted, so I inferred that the block was partial and was still in place.

We do think that it caused some problems at the submit node. Knowing now that the block was short-lived, this perhaps indicates more of a problem with Condor than anything else. It should be able to handle 10 minutes of effectively being off the network, but doesn't seem to have. We should perhaps react by setting up a watcher to restart Condor services when threads go into zombie state.

That said, a whitelist might be a good solution as well.  Presumably this will prevent anyone external to IU from having this problem with the specific osg-flock.grid.iu.edu service?

It would also be good to know whether all alerts trigger only 10-minute blocks, or whether some are permanent — flocking peers would want to be aware of those that are permanent.

Hi Rob,
First, let me note that the block in question was 10 minutes in length. So this
host should not have any problems connecting at this point. I noticed that the
same host was blocked again about two days later. My apologies for the impact
in service.
This block was caused by the host in question triggering a high number of reset
and "port unreachable" type errors in a short window of time. That looks a lot
like the network artifacts of a port scanner. Unfortunately, it can trigger
falsely on services that negotiate a lot of connections, such as OSG services.
I can add a whitelist entry for the IU IP that was the target, in this case it
was 129.79.53.179. Please note that the whitelist will only cover this specific
alert, although this appears to be the only one causing issues for your
service.
Do you have other hosts you think should be added to this whitelist? I'm
hesitant to whitelist external IPs so I'd prefer to focus on the local services
you offer. If you have full CIDR blocks to recommend, that would also be an
option.

    It's hard to say for certain, not knowing what exactly was blocked.  I can ping to osg-flock, for what that's worth.  We should be able to tell over time whether jobs are going to OSG. The letter our abuse team got mentioned port scanning, but I'm not sure why UITSO believes this was port scanning behavior.  To us it looks like Condor.  We had a very productive day yesterday, or would have had — more jobs than usual.

This is the only log data we got from UITSO:
2014-06-26T06:05:31+00:00 swill 1403762730.444511   -   -   -   -   -   -   -   -   -   Scan::Port_Scan 192.170.227.195 scanned at least 53 unique ports of host 129.79.53.179 in 2m57s remote  192.170.227.195 129.79.53.179   -   -   swill2-2    Notice::ACTION_LOG  3600.000000 F   -   -   -   -   -

The actual flow data isn't here, so I can't infer what the pattern is that looks like a port scan. If someone did scan from our submit node we'd like to know more details so that we can act on it. We have (partial) flow data from our security office, indicating that most activity was between ports 9700 and 10000, and consisted of a minimum of 31.0KB per flow, up to several MB, which to me indicates an application with a fully-open connection, not a half-open (SYN/ACK/RST) port-knock.

Also, can we get details on what the block was? We'd like to confirm this was the root cause of what we've seen and know how to confirm it's resolved.

My real interest here is just in obtaining agreement that this is normal, not a risk, and finding a way to ensure that it doesn't happen again to us or to any other sites that flock to this server unless it's truly a security risk. (OSG/XSEDE is probably not at risk for this since it's located within the IU perimeter.) GOC team, if it's better for me to work directly with UITSO on this please let me know.

Thanks all!
